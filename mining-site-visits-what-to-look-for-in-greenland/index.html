Mining Site Visits: What to Look For in Greenland

By Intel Desk · 2026-03-23

site visitgreenlanddue diligencefield assessment

Site visits are a critical component of mining investment due diligence. In Greenland, the logistics are more challenging than in most mining jurisdictions, but the information gained from a well-planned visit can be decisive. This guide covers what to look for, how to get there, and how to maximize the value of a Greenland site visit.

When to Visit

Timing: Greenland's field season runs from approximately June to September. Outside this window, access is extremely limited and weather conditions may be hazardous.

June-July: Best weather, longest daylight hours (24-hour daylight in some areas), good for assessing terrain and vegetation

August: Still accessible, shorter daylight hours

September: End of reliable field season, risk of early winter weather

Logistics

Getting to Greenland:

  • Air Greenland operates flights from Copenhagen to Nuuk and other destinations
  • Icelandair flies from Reykjavik to Kulusuk and Nuuk seasonally
  • No direct flights from North America

    • Getting to the site:

  • Skaergaard: Charter flight from Iceland or Kulusuk to a landing strip near the project (if available), or helicopter
  • Remote exploration sites: Charter helicopter or boat

    • Cost: A Greenland site visit typically costs US$5,000-15,000 per person including flights, accommodation, and charter transport.

    Planning: Allow 7-10 days for a comprehensive visit, including travel time and weather contingency.

    What to Observe

    Geological exposure:

  • Outcrop quality and continuity
  • Visible mineralization (sulfide veins, disseminated sulfides, alteration zones)
  • Structural features (faulting, folding, intrusion contacts)
  • Compare what you see on the ground to cross-sections and resource models

    • Drilling evidence:

  • Drill core storage and condition
  • Core logging quality and consistency
  • Sample preparation and security procedures
  • Assay database integrity

    • Infrastructure potential:

  • Terrain suitability for roads, port, airstrip, processing plant
  • Water sources and drainage patterns
  • Distance to potential marine terminal sites
  • Permafrost conditions and foundation suitability

    • Environmental baseline:

  • Current environmental condition (vegetation, water, wildlife)
  • Proximity to sensitive areas (bird nesting, marine mammal habitats, fishing areas)
  • Evidence of existing environmental disturbance (historical exploration, glacial erosion)
  • Drainage patterns and potential contamination pathways

    • Community Assessment

    Local attitudes:

  • Meet with community leaders and residents
  • Assess support for, opposition to, or ambivalence about mining development
  • Understand local concerns and priorities
  • Evaluate the quality of the company's community engagement to date

    • Community infrastructure:

  • Existing housing, schools, health facilities
  • Utility services (power, water, telecommunications)
  • Transportation (port, airfield, roads)
  • Assess whether mining infrastructure could benefit the community

    • Management Assessment

    Team quality:

  • Meet the project management team
  • Assess technical competence and Arctic operational experience
  • Evaluate communication style and transparency
  • Understand the company's approach to environmental and social management

    • Safety culture:

  • Observe safety practices during the visit
  • Review safety statistics and incident records
  • Assess emergency response planning for Arctic conditions

    • Document Review on Site

    Request and review on-site:

  • Complete drill hole database and assay records
  • Exploration and resource model files
  • Environmental baseline study data
  • Community consultation records
  • Permit applications and correspondence with regulators
  • Financial model assumptions and sensitivity analysis

    • Common Pitfalls

  • **Weather delays**: Greenland weather can ground flights for days. Build weather contingency into your schedule
  • **Confirmation bias**: It is easy to see what you want to see on a site visit. Bring a critical eye and compare observations to published data
  • **Social desirability**: Community members may tell visitors what they think they want to hear. Seek diverse perspectives
  • **Incomplete picture**: A one-time visit provides a snapshot. Seasonal variation, multi-year weather patterns, and long-term environmental trends require data analysis beyond the visit

    • Virtual Site Visit Alternative

    When physical travel is not feasible, consider:

  • **Video walkthroughs**: Request high-quality video of key project locations
  • **Drone footage**: Aerial video provides excellent terrain and infrastructure context
  • **Virtual meetings**: Schedule calls with management, community leaders, and technical consultants
  • **Data room access**: Ensure access to complete exploration and environmental databases

    • What to Take Away

    After a Greenland site visit, you should be able to answer:

  • Does the geological evidence support the published resource model?
  • Is the terrain suitable for the proposed mining method and infrastructure?
  • What are the environmental and social risks, and how well is the company managing them?
  • Does the management team inspire confidence in their ability to execute in Arctic conditions?
  • What are the community attitudes toward the project, and is the company's engagement credible?

    • Conclusion

    A well-planned Greenland site visit is one of the most valuable due diligence tools available to mining investors. The logistics are challenging but manageable, and the information gained from direct observation can confirm or challenge the investment thesis in ways that desktop analysis cannot. For Skaergaard, a site visit would provide direct evidence of the geological model, terrain suitability, and community context that are central to the investment case.
