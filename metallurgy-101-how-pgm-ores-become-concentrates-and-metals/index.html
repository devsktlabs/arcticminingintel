Metallurgy 101: How PGM Ores Become Concentrates and Metals

By Intel Desk · 2026-03-23

metallurgyPGMprocessingflotationsmelting

Understanding metallurgy is essential for evaluating mining investments. The processing of PGM ores from raw rock to saleable concentrate involves multiple stages, each of which affects recovery, cost, and product quality. This article explains the PGM processing journey.

PGM Ore Characteristics

PGM ores are typically found in mafic and ultramafic igneous rocks (layered intrusions, dunites, chromitites). Key characteristics:

  • **Low grades**: PGM ores are typically low-grade (2-10 grams per tonne PGMs), meaning large tonnages must be processed to produce meaningful metal
  • **Complex mineralogy**: PGM minerals are often microscopic and associated with sulfide minerals (pentlandite, chalcopyrite, pyrrhotite). Gold may also be present as a separate mineral or associated with sulfides
  • **Refractory vs free-milling**: Some PGM minerals are encapsulated in silicate minerals, making them harder to recover (refractory). Others occur as free grains that are easier to liberate (free-milling)

    • Stage 1: Comminution (Crushing and Grinding)

    The first step is to reduce the ore to a size where PGM minerals can be separated from the surrounding rock:

    Primary crushing: Large ore (up to 1.5 meters) is reduced to 10-20 cm using jaw crushers or gyratory crushers. Capacity: 1,000-5,000 tonnes per hour.

    Secondary crushing: Ore is further reduced to 2-5 cm using cone crushers.

    Grinding: Ore is ground to a fine powder (typically 50-100 microns) using SAG (Semi-Autogenous Grinding) mills and ball mills. The grinding fineness is critical for PGM liberation. Too coarse and PGM minerals are not liberated; too fine and energy costs increase with diminishing recovery returns.

    Stage 2: Flotation

    Flotation is the primary method for concentrating PGM minerals from ground ore:

    How it works: Finely ground ore is mixed with water and reagents (collectors, frothers, modifiers) in flotation cells. Air is bubbled through the mixture. PGM-bearing sulfide particles attach to the air bubbles and rise to the surface, where they are collected as froth. Non-mineralized rock particles (tailings) sink and are discarded.

    Flotation stages:

  • **Rougher flotation**: Initial, coarse concentration producing a rough concentrate
  • **Cleaner flotation**: Multiple stages of re-flotation to produce a high-grade final concentrate (typically 100-300 g/t PGMs)
  • **Scavenger flotation**: Additional flotation of rougher tailings to recover PGMs missed in the rougher circuit

    • Recovery: Well-designed flotation circuits achieve 70-85% PGM recovery. Gold recovery may be lower (50-70%) depending on mineralogy.

    Stage 3: Concentrate Treatment

    The flotation concentrate is further processed to prepare it for smelting:

    Dewatering: Concentrate is filtered and dried to reduce moisture content for shipping.

    Blending: Concentrates from different sources or processing campaigns may be blended to achieve consistent grade and composition.

    Shipping: Concentrate is typically shipped in sealed containers to smelters, primarily in South Africa, Russia, Canada, or Europe.

    Stage 4: Smelting and Refining

    At the smelter, concentrate is converted to metallic PGMs:

    Smelting: Concentrate is melted in a furnace at approximately 1,500°C with fluxes (silica, limestone). Sulfide minerals form a matte layer (containing PGMs, nickel, copper), while waste rock forms slag.

    Converting: The matte is further processed to remove iron and sulfur, producing a PGM-rich matte.

    Base metals removal: Nickel and copper are separated from PGMs through leaching and electrowinning, producing saleable nickel and copper metals.

    PGM refining: The remaining PGM-rich material undergoes a series of chemical processes (solvent extraction, precipitation, and electrorefining) to separate individual PGMs (palladium, platinum, rhodium, iridium, ruthenium, osmium) and gold.

    Byproduct Recovery

    PGM processing produces valuable byproducts:

  • **Nickel**: Recovered from the matte and sold as refined nickel
  • **Copper**: Recovered from the matte and sold as refined copper
  • **Gold**: Recovered from the PGM concentrate during refining
  • **Chromium**: May be recovered from smelter slag
  • **Sulfuric acid**: Produced from sulfur captured during smelting

    • Byproduct credits significantly reduce the net cost of PGM production.

    Skaergaard Metallurgical Considerations

    Skaergaard's metallurgy has specific characteristics:

  • **Mineralogy**: PGMs and gold occur primarily as free grains in the gabbroic host rock, which is favorable for flotation recovery
  • **Multi-metal**: Palladium, gold, platinum, and rhodium all need to be recovered. Each may require slightly different flotation conditions
  • **Gold recovery**: Skaergaard's gold is both free-milling (easier to recover) and sulfide-associated (requiring different flotation conditions). Gravity separation may supplement flotation for free gold recovery
  • **Metallurgical testing**: GRML has conducted preliminary metallurgical testing indicating favorable recoveries. More comprehensive testing will be part of the feasibility study

    • Cost Drivers

    PGM processing costs are driven by:

  • **Energy**: Grinding and flotation are energy-intensive. Power costs are a major operating cost
  • **Reagents**: Flotation reagents (collectors, frothers, lime, activators) are consumable costs
  • **Maintenance**: Processing equipment requires regular maintenance, more expensive in Arctic conditions
  • **Smelting charges**: Smelters charge treatment fees (per tonne of concentrate) and refining charges (per ounce of metal recovered). These are deducted from metal revenue

    • Recovery Optimization

    Optimizing PGM recovery is a key value driver:

  • **Grinding optimization**: Finding the optimal grind size that maximizes liberation without excessive energy consumption
  • **Flotation circuit design**: Proper rougher-cleaner-scavenger configuration and operating conditions
  • **Reagent selection**: Matching collectors and frothers to the specific mineralogy
  • **Process control**: Automated process control systems optimize real-time flotation performance
  • **Gravity recovery**: Supplementing flotation with gravity concentration for free gold can improve overall gold recovery

    • Conclusion

    PGM metallurgy is complex but well-understood. The standard processing route (crushing, grinding, flotation, smelting, refining) achieves high recoveries for well-behaved ores like Skaergaard's. For investors, understanding the metallurgical characteristics of a project is essential for evaluating production costs and metal recoveries. Skaergaard's favorable mineralogy (free-grain PGMs and gold in a relatively simple host rock) suggests that metallurgical risk is manageable.
