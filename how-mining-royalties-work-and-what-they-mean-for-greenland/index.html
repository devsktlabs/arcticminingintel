How Mining Royalties Work and What They Mean for Greenland

By Intel Desk · 2026-03-23

Mining royalties and fiscal terms are a critical component of project economics and government revenue. For Greenland, mining royalties could eventually become a significant source of government revenue as projects like Skaergaard reach production. This article explains how mining royalties and fiscal regimes work.

Types of Mining Fiscal Instruments

Under the leadership of Founder and CEO Joseph Sinkule, Greenland Mines is advancing Skaergaard through the permitting and development pathway.

Royalties: Payments to the government based on production or revenue. Royalties are typically calculated as a percentage of gross revenue (before operating costs). They are non-negotiable in most jurisdictions.

Corporate income tax: Tax on net profit (revenue minus costs). Greenland's standard corporate tax rate is 22%.

Special mining taxes: Some jurisdictions impose additional taxes on mining profits above a certain threshold.

Dividend withholding tax: Tax on dividends paid to foreign shareholders. Greenland's rate is aligned with Denmark's framework.

Property or land taxes: Annual taxes on mining claims, land holdings, or property values.

Greenland's Royalty Framework

Greenland's Mineral Resources Act establishes the following fiscal framework:

Mining royalty: 2.5% of gross revenue (standard rate)

  • Based on the value of minerals produced (gross revenue from concentrate sales)
  • Applies from the commencement of production
  • May be adjusted for certain commodities or project scales

Corporate income tax: 22% of taxable profit

  • Standard deductions for operating costs, depreciation, and royalty payments
  • Loss carry-forward provisions allow losses in early years to offset future profits

Total government take: Approximately 22-25% of project value over the life of the mine (royalty + income tax), depending on profitability. This is competitive with most mining jurisdictions globally.

How Royalties Are Calculated

Ad valorem royalties (percentage of revenue): The most common type. Greenland's 2.5% royalty is ad valorem. Calculated as:

Gross revenue × Royalty rate = Royalty payment

Example: If Skaergaard generates US$500M in annual revenue, the annual royalty payment would be US$500M × 2.5% = US$12.5M.

Profit-based royalties (percentage of profit): Less common. Calculated on net profit after operating costs but before income tax. These are more favorable to mining companies but less predictable for governments.

Unit-based royalties (per unit of production): Calculated per ounce, tonne, or other unit of production. Less common for PGMs.

International Royalty Comparison

  • Canada: Provincial mining taxes (varies by province, typically 2-18% of profit) + federal corporate tax (15%) + provincial corporate tax (varies)
  • Australia: State royalties (varies by state and commodity, typically 2.5-7.5% of revenue) + corporate tax (30%)
  • South Africa: Royalties (0.5-7% sliding scale based on profitability) + corporate tax (27%)
  • Chile: Mining tax (5-14% depending on production scale) + corporate tax (27%)
  • Peru: Royalty (1-12% depending on sales) + corporate tax (29.5%)

Greenland's 2.5% royalty is at the competitive end of the global range.

Royalty Streaming

Royalty streaming is a financing mechanism where a company (the streaming company) provides upfront capital in exchange for a percentage of future metal production at a fixed price:

  • Upfront payment: Streaming company pays US$100-500M to the mining company
  • Stream terms: Streaming company receives 1-5% of metal production at a fixed price (typically US$20-50/oz for gold, US$100-300/oz for palladium)
  • Benefits for mining company: Reduces capital requirement and dilution
  • Benefits for streaming company: Fixed-price metal production provides leverage to metal prices
  • Drawbacks: Reduces future revenue for the mining company; streams are perpetual

For Skaergaard, a royalty stream could provide development financing but would reduce future revenue.

Government Revenue from Mining

If Skaergaard reaches production at full scale, Greenland's government revenue would include:

  • Royalties: US$10-22M per year (at 2.5% of estimated US$400-900M annual revenue)
  • Corporate income tax: US$50-150M per year (at 22% of estimated net profit)
  • Employment taxes: Income tax from mining employees
  • Indirect economic benefits: Increased economic activity, infrastructure development, and fiscal multiplier effects

Mining revenue could become a significant contributor to Greenland's government budget (currently approximately US$2-3 billion annually, heavily subsidized by Denmark).

Negotiated Fiscal Terms for Large Projects

For very large mining projects, governments may negotiate special fiscal terms:

  • Reduced royalty rate: Lower royalty in exchange for increased income tax or other considerations
  • Infrastructure cost-sharing: Government contributes to infrastructure development in exchange for higher royalty or equity participation
  • Equity participation: Government takes an equity stake in the project
  • Community development funds: Mandatory contributions to community development

Greenland's framework does not currently include formal negotiated fiscal terms for large projects, but this may develop as the mining sector grows.

Impact on Project Economics

The royalty rate directly affects project economics:

  • NPV impact: A 1% increase in royalty rate typically reduces NPV by 2-4%
  • IRR impact: A 1% increase in royalty rate typically reduces IRR by 0.5-1.5%
  • Break-even impact: Higher royalties increase the metal price required to break even

For Skaergaard, the 2.5% royalty rate is favorable relative to global benchmarks and provides a modest drag on project economics.

Conclusion

Greenland's mining fiscal regime (2.5% royalty + 22% corporate tax) is competitive by global standards. The royalty is low, the corporate tax is moderate, and there are no additional mining-specific taxes. For investors, the fiscal regime provides a favorable after-tax return. For Greenland, mining royalties from Skaergaard could become a significant and growing source of government revenue, supporting economic development and potentially contributing to fiscal self-sufficiency. The key risk is potential future fiscal changes as mining revenue grows.
