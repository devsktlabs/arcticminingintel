Tailings Management in Arctic Environments: Challenges and Solutions

By Intel Desk · 2026-03-23

tailingsarcticenvironmentalmining waste

Tailings management is one of the most critical environmental challenges in mining. In Arctic environments, the challenges are amplified by permafrost, extreme cold, and the sensitivity of Arctic ecosystems. This article examines the specific challenges of Arctic tailings management and the solutions being developed.

What Are Tailings?

Tailings are the waste material remaining after ore processing. They consist of finely ground rock, process water, and residual reagents. Key characteristics:

  • Volume: Large. A typical mining operation generates millions of tonnes of tailings per year
  • Water content: Tailings are typically stored as a slurry (25-40% solids by weight)
  • Chemical composition: Varies by ore type. May contain residual processing reagents, heavy metals, or acid-generating sulfides
  • Long-term liability: Tailings facilities must be maintained and monitored in perpetuity after mine closure

Arctic-Specific Challenges

Permafrost:

  • Building on permafrost is complex. Tailings facilities can thaw underlying permafrost, causing foundation instability
  • Permafrost thaw can cause differential settlement, cracking of containment structures, and potential release
  • Solution: Use thermosiphons (passive cooling devices) to maintain frozen ground conditions. Design foundations to accommodate potential thaw

Freeze-thaw cycles:

  • Cyclic freezing and thawing can damage containment structures (embankments, liners)
  • Frost heave can shift structures and breach containment
  • Solution: Design structures for freeze-thaw resistance. Use frost-resistant materials and flexible liners

Water management:

  • Arctic water bodies are highly sensitive to contamination (low temperatures reduce natural degradation)
  • Ice cover prevents natural aeration of tailings ponds
  • Seasonal flow variations (spring breakup, winter freeze) complicate water management
  • Solution: Closed-circuit water recycling to minimize discharge. Engineered treatment systems for any discharge

Remote location:

  • Emergency response capability is limited by distance and weather
  • Construction materials must be transported from distant sources
  • Inspection and monitoring frequency may be limited by weather and access
  • Solution: Robust design with multiple safety factors. Remote monitoring systems. Emergency response planning specific to Arctic conditions

Seismic risk:

  • Some Arctic regions are seismically active (including parts of Greenland)
  • Frozen tailings may be more susceptible to liquefaction during earthquakes
  • Solution: Seismic design criteria for tailings facilities. Dynamic stability analysis

Tailings Storage Methods

Conventional tailings dams: Earth and rock embankments built progressively as the facility grows. The most common method globally but requires careful design for Arctic conditions.

Thickened tailings: Tailings are dewatered to a higher solids content (50-70%) before disposal. Reduces water storage volume and improves stability. Growing adoption in Arctic operations.

Dry stacking: Tailings are filtered and stacked in piles with minimal water content. The most environmentally responsible option but higher capital and operating costs. Increasingly preferred for new Arctic projects.

Paste backfill: Processed tailings are mixed with binders and returned to underground mine voids. Eliminates surface tailings storage for underground operations.

Regulatory Requirements

Greenland's tailings management requirements (consistent with Danish and EU norms) include:

  • Tailings management plan: Comprehensive plan covering design, construction, operation, monitoring, closure, and post-closure
  • Geotechnical assessment: Independent geotechnical review of facility design and stability
  • Water quality monitoring: Regular monitoring of water quality in and around the facility
  • Financial assurance: Bond or guarantee sufficient to cover closure and post-closure costs
  • Closure plan: Detailed plan for decommissioning and long-term monitoring

Best Practices for Arctic Tailings Management

  • Design for climate change: Incorporate climate change projections into facility design (warming temperatures, increased precipitation, permafrost thaw)
  • Thickened tailings or dry stacking: Prefer methods that reduce water content and improve stability
  • Multiple containment: Use composite liner systems with primary and secondary containment
  • Water balance management: Maintain a positive water balance (no net discharge) to minimize environmental risk
  • Progressive reclamation: Begin reclamation of completed sections while the facility is still operating
  • Remote monitoring: Install automated monitoring systems for water levels, seepage, and structural integrity

The Mount Polley Lesson

The 2014 Mount Polley tailings dam failure in British Columbia (a non-Arctic jurisdiction) resulted in the release of 25 million cubic meters of tailings and water into nearby waterways. The incident led to:

  • A comprehensive review of tailings dam safety in Canada
  • Adoption of best available technology (BAT) requirements, including elimination of water-retaining tailings dams where possible
  • Increased focus on dry stacking and filtered tailings

These lessons apply with even greater force in Arctic environments, where the consequences of a tailings failure could be more severe and recovery more difficult.

Skaergaard Tailings Considerations

For Skaergaard, tailings management will be a major environmental and cost consideration:

  • Location: East Greenland's terrain offers potential tailings storage sites in valleys or depressions near the processing plant
  • Permafrost: Active layer thickness and permafrost depth at potential tailings sites must be thoroughly investigated
  • Water management: A closed-circuit water system with zero discharge is likely required
  • Method: Thickened tailings or dry stacking should be seriously considered, despite higher cost, given the environmental sensitivity and remote location
  • Long-term stability: The tailings facility must be designed for long-term stability under changing climate conditions

Conclusion

Arctic tailings management is more challenging and more important than in temperate-zone mining. Permafrost, freeze-thaw cycles, water management, and remote location all add complexity and cost. The industry trend toward thickened tailings and dry stacking is particularly relevant for Arctic operations. For Skaergaard, a robust tailings management design that accounts for Arctic-specific challenges and climate change will be essential for environmental compliance and social license.
